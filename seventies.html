Top Comedy Movies of 1970s

Blazingsaddles

Blazingsaddles

Blazing Saddles" is more than just a comedy with a heart; it's a film that fearlessly tackles enduring truths about race, gender, politics, and more with unmatched boldness. Mel Brooks, alongside talents like Richard Pryor and Gene Wilder, crafted a movie that not only serves as the quintessential Western comedy but also as a timeless commentary on America's complexities. Its brilliant writing, stellar performances, and daring humor remain relevant, echoing the societal struggles of both the past and present. As a film that also reflects on the nature of filmmaking itself, it stands out in Brooks' impressive repertoire, which includes classics like "The Producers" and "Young Frankenstein."

Monty Python

Monty Python

Plenty of Python fans will fist-fight you over which sketch or character is their favorite (and therefore best), but there’s little question that Holy Grail is the British outfit’s most fiendishly inspired full-length outing. It’s chiefly concerned with slathering Python’s devastating wit and surreal ideas over implicitly Big-Picture topics like mortality, the innate dignity of the soul, political repression, and more. It’s got an urgency born from its production particulars (having been conceived between seasons 3 and 4 of their TV series), a relatively paltry budget, and the fact that the Pythons successfully translated their fearless experimentation from television to the big screen. This Arthurian fable is densely packed with superb ideas executed flawlessly.

Annie Hall

Annie Hall

Comedian Alvy Singer (Woody Allen) examines the rise and fall of his relationship with struggling nightclub singer Annie Hall (Diane Keaton). Speaking directly to the audience in front of a bare background, Singer reflects briefly on his childhood and his early adult years before settling in to tell the story of how he and Annie met, fell in love, and struggled with the obstacles of modern romance, mixing surreal fantasy sequences with small moments of emotional drama.

Smokey and the Bandit

Smokey and the Bandit

Smokey and the Bandit is a 1977 American action comedy film starring Burt Reynolds, Sally Field, Jackie Gleason, Jerry Reed, Pat McCormick, Paul Williams and Mike Henry. The film is the first installment of the Smokey and the Bandit trilogy as the start in the Smokey and the Bandit franchise.

The Jerk

The Jerk

Finding a more likable lead than Steve Martin would have been impossible for this fish-out-of-water tale, which was inspired by a single line in Martin’s standup act and traces Navin R. Johnson’s rise from a poor “black” child to a wealthy, accidental inventor – and back to the literal gutter again. Like Being There, the lead’s innocence and simplicity is contrasted to great effect with the cold, hard world around him. Palpable chemistry between Martin and Bernadette Peters elevates what could have been a casually sketched love story into something classic, dignified, and sweet. Martin is eager to please in his first starring role, never turning up his nose at a dumb bit of humor.
